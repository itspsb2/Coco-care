# Coconut Caterpillar and its Control

### Official CRI Source
- Source ID: B2
- Category: Pests & Diseases
- Title: Coconut Caterpillar and its Control
- PDF: https://cri.gov.lk/wp-content/uploads/2021/10/b2.pdf

### Details
The Coconut Caterpillar, *Opisina arenosella* (earlier known as *Nephantis serinopa*), is a serious pest of coconut prevalent in many coconut growing areas. Outbreaks normally occur between February and October. Extensive leaf damage could lead to yield losses.

##### Symptoms
The damage is caused by the caterpillars feeding on the tissues of the lower surface of leaves. Freshly damaged tissues appear green and later turn brown and dry up.
- Infested palms can be easily recognized by the dried-up patches on leaflets of the lower whorls of leaves.
- In an outbreak, a large number of leaves are affected, and sometimes the epidermis of nuts is also attacked.
- On examination, galleries constructed with small pieces of leaf tissue and excreted material (in which caterpillars live) can be seen on the lower surface.

##### Description of the pest
- **Adult moth:** Ashy brown in colour and about 12 mm long.
- **Eggs:** Females lay eggs in clusters on the lower surface of leaves.
- **Caterpillar (Larva):** Hatchlings have pinkish bodies and gradually turn to a greenish-brown colour with development. The head is brownish black. A full-grown caterpillar is about 20 mm long and has dark brown longitudinal stripes on the dorsal surface of the body. They feed on superficial leaf tissues while living inside the galleries. Several galleries can be seen on one leaflet. The larval stage lasts for 30-40 days.
- **Pupa:** Spent inside a cocoon and lasts for about 14-21 days.
- **Adult life:** Adults live about 7 days.
- **Total life cycle:** Takes about 2-2½ months.

##### Control
At low population levels, the pest is kept under control by natural enemies. However, low night temperatures from October to February are unfavorable to these natural enemies, disturbing the natural balance and leading to outbreaks. Early detection is essential.

1.  **Low Infestation (less than 30 palms affected):** Cut 5-6 infested fronds and burn them. Do not remove more than 5-6 fronds from a palm. If adult moths are present, cut the fronds only after 2 weeks (check for moths by tapping infested leaves). Laboratory-bred parasitoids should be released after removing the fronds.
2.  **Large Infestation:** Regular release of lab-bred parasitoids is necessary.
3.  **Outbreak Situations:** Parasitoids alone are not enough. Apply Monocrotophos 60% by trunk injection. For seedlings and young palms, spray Marshal 20 SC (4 ml in 1 liter of water) on infested leaves.

**Action:** Notify the Coconut Development Officer (CDO) immediately. The CDO will inspect the plantation and assess pest and parasitoid populations to provide advice.

##### Release of parasitoids
Several types of parasitoids are bred at the Coconut Research Institute (CRI) for release. Since release should only occur after pest assessment, inform the CDO to obtain required information and report to the CRI, Lunuwila.
- Tubes containing parasitoids can be collected from the CRI or obtained by mail.
- Release them near infested palms as soon as possible.
- Follow the instructions sent with the consignment.
- Return the boxes with empty tubes without delay.
- Do not light night fires after parasitoids have been released.

##### Trunk injection with systemic insecticide
Monocrotophos 60% is highly toxic and banned for general use, except in coconut plantations as a trunk injection. It is issued to growers under the supervision of the CDO. The CDO will provide further advice if insecticide application is required.

**Note:** All insecticides are toxic and should be handled with care.

### Advisory Circular No B 5: COCONUT SCALE AND ITS CONTROL

The Coconut Scale (*Aspidiotus destructor*) is prevalent in all coconut growing areas. Considerable damage occurs only during outbreaks, which favour dry weather and often occur during prolonged droughts, diminishing with the onset of rains.

#### Nature of damage and identification
Damage is caused by females and immature males feeding on sap from the lower surface of leaflets. The damage initiates from the lower whorl and spreads upwards.
- **Identification:** Infested palms show yellow patches on leaflets. Closer examination reveals numerous yellow spots (feeding marks) on the lamina. Scale insects appear as a yellowish-white encrustation on the underside.
- **Fingernail test:** Running a fingernail over the encrustation yields a watery matter if alive, or a scaly fluff if dead.
- **Severe Infestation:** Entire leaves become yellow and dry off. Flower spikes and nuts may also be infested. Seedling infestation can cause stunted growth.

**Note:** Yellowing should not be confused with mineral deficiency. Scale insect yellowing is easily identified by close examination.

#### Description of the pest
- With a hand lens, they look like a collection of fried eggs (bull's eye) with a yellow central area surrounded by a transparent waxy covering.
- **Eggs & Crawlers:** Females lay eggs under the covering. These hatch into minute nymphs called crawlers, which are not visible to the naked eye. Crawlers move about until they find a suitable spot, shed their legs, form a waxy covering, and settle.
- **Sexual Development:** Males (after 30-35 days) develop wings and fly to mate. Females remain sedentary until they die in 34-40 days.

#### Control measures
Notify the Coconut Development Officer (CDO) if the pest is detected. The CDO will inspect and provide advice.

**1. Cultural Control:**
- If only a few leaves are infested, cut and burn them.
- For seedlings, crush the insects or remove affected parts.

**2. Biological Control:**
Natural enemies include ladybird beetles and a parasitic wasp.
- **Ladybird Beetles:** Two common species are *Chilocorus nigritus* (black, roundish, size of a pepper seed) and *Pullus xerampelinus* (slightly smaller, roundish, brown). Both adults and larvae are voracious feeders on scale insects and can check outbreaks.
- **Wasp:** *Aphytis chrysomphali* is parasitic on the scale insects.
- Officers from the CCB will assess the abundance of these natural enemies. If numbers are sufficient, no chemical control is needed. If numbers are low, monitor monthly. If they do not build up, chemical control is considered. Note that yellowed leaves will not regain colour, but new leaves will be green once the infestation is controlled.

**3. Chemical Control:**
Recommended only if biological control is ineffective. Seek advice from the CCB before attempting chemical measures.
- **For seedlings:** Spray Dimethoate 40% or Chlorpyrifos 40% at 20-40 ml in 10 liters of water using a knapsack sprayer.
- **For adult palms (outbreak):** Inject 8 ml of Monocrotophos 60% (under CDO supervision as it is highly toxic).

Chemical control is rarely necessary. If required, the CRI can help with application, provided the grower pays for the insecticide, fuel, and labour.

**Note:** All insecticides are toxic and should be handled with care.

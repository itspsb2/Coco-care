### Advisory Circular No B 16: COCONUT MITE AND ITS MANAGEMENT

The Coconut mite, scientifically known as *Aceria guerreronis*, is an introduced pest to Sri Lanka. It was first reported in the Kalpitiya peninsula in late 1997. Currently, it is occurring in Northern, North-central, Western, and Eastern provinces, and in the Gampaha, Matale, and Hambantota districts. The pest damages the nuts.

#### Nature and Identification of Damage
The coconut mite is microscopic in size and cannot be seen by the naked eye. It is worm-like in shape and lives in large aggregated colonies beneath the perianth of nuts, from just after fertilization up to the harvesting stage. The young nuts of 3-6 months old are most vulnerable to the pest. The mites feed on the meristematic tissues beneath the perianth.
- **Initial symptom:** A yellowish-white triangular patch with the base at the level of the perianth.
- **Progression:** The patches become necrotic and gradually enlarge with the continued feeding of the mites.
- **Result:** The nuts fail to develop normally and become deformed or smaller in size. Sometimes "Y" shape deep cracks develop at the level of the perianth. Severely damaged nuts may fall prematurely.

#### Management of the pest
A successful and sustainable management method has not yet been developed. The present strategy is to reduce the damage and spread as much as possible.

**Reduce Spread:**
The pest spreads mainly by wind and infested materials. Avoid transporting infested fresh nuts and husks to un-infested areas. Infested material kept over one month after harvest are free from live mites and could be transported. Infested fallen immature nuts in the estates should be collected and burnt or buried.

**Reduce Damage:**
1.  If the infestation is reported in a few palms for the first time, cut and burn all nuts in the infested palms.
2.  Apply **30% used engine oil** on nuts of infested palms. This is recommended for infestations in boundary areas, home gardens, or selected heavily infested palms. If properly applied, it kills almost all mites, providing curative and preventive action. Apply at 2-monthly intervals to newly developing untreated nuts until damage is reduced. Do not repeat on already treated nuts.
3.  **Interim recommendation:** Apply **palm/vegetable oil and sulphur mixture**. If properly applied, over 90% of mites can be killed. The mixture can be sprayed from the ground level using a modified knapsack sprayer. Apply at 3-4 month intervals, preferably in dry months (January, April, July), to bunches starting from the newly opened inflorescence up to 6-7 months old. Unlike engine oil, this mixture is not deleterious to natural enemies.

#### Preparation and Application of Chemicals

**A. Used Engine Oil Mixture (Ingredients for 1 litre):**
- Water: 700 ml (3.5 cups)
- Used (waste) engine oil: 300 ml (1.5 cups)
- Soap powder/liquid: 6 g (1 tablespoon)
- Wheat flour: 6 g (1 tablespoon)
- *Ensure used engine oil is not mixed with kerosene oil.*

**Method:**
1.  Add soap powder/liquid to water and stir well.
2.  Add flour to the soap water mixture and stir well to form a homogenous mixture.
3.  Add used engine oil to the soap and flour mixture and mix thoroughly.
4.  Transfer to a plastic bottle.

**Application:**
- Climb the tree with the bottle. Thoroughly shake and pour a little mixture on the palm of the hand (wear rubber gloves). Rub the mixture on the perianth and the nut surface adjoining the perianth of all nuts of 2nd to 6th old bunches from the opened inflorescence.
- The smallest button nut to treat is about 4-5 cm long.
- One person can treat 30-40 palms in a day. One litre is sufficient for 8-10 palms.

**B. Palm/vegetable oil and sulphur mixture (Ingredients for 1 litre):**
- Palm oil: 200 ml (1 cup)
- Water: 800 ml (4 cups)
- Soap powder: 12 g (2 tablespoons)
- Wettable sulphur 80%: 5 g (1 tablespoon)

**Method:**
1.  Mix water, soap powder, and sulphur.
2.  Add palm oil to the mixture and mix thoroughly.
- 1 litre of the solution is enough to spray one palm.
- **Application:** It is not necessary to climb the palm. Modify a knapsack sprayer by inserting a flexible plastic tube between the tap and the lance. Tie the lance to a bamboo or aluminum pole to reach up to 40 feet high trees.

#### Future Strategies
Chemical methods provide only short-term control. Priority is being given to developing biological control methods using natural enemies (predatory mites and an entomopathogenic fungus).
